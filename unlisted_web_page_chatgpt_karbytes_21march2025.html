– Macroscopic systems are made of trillions of quantum particles.

2. Quantum Mechanics & the Many-Worlds Hypothesis (MWH)

What is Many-Worlds?

The Many-Worlds Interpretation (MWI) says:

– Every time a quantum measurement is made and multiple outcomes are possible, all outcomes actually happen‚Äîbut in separate, non-interacting branches of the universe.

So when you flip a quantum coin:

– One version of “you” sees heads.

– Another version of “you” sees tails.

– Both outcomes are equally real, but they exist in parallel universes.

This removes the need for a wavefunction ‚Äúcollapse.‚Äù Instead, the universe splits into branches‚Äîone for each possible outcome.

Relation to Quantum Mechanics:

MWI is one of several interpretations of quantum mechanics. The math stays the same; it‚Äôs the philosophical framing that differs.

– Standard Copenhagen Interpretation: Wavefunction collapses upon observation.

– Many-Worlds: Wavefunction never collapses‚Äîobservation causes branching.

Why MWI Appeals to Some Physicists:

– It‚Äôs deterministic: The wavefunction evolves smoothly; no randomness is injected.

– It‚Äôs unitary: No special rule needed for measurement (it‚Äôs all Schr√∂dinger‚Äôs equation).

But it‚Äôs untestable so far‚Äîthese alternate universes don‚Äôt interact, so we can‚Äôt (yet) observe them.

To Bring It All Together:

– Quantum mechanics predicts probabilities, not specific future events, especially on the macroscopic scale.

– The Many-Worlds Hypothesis is one way to explain quantum probabilities: all possible outcomes do happen, just not all in the same branch of reality.

– So in MWI, every macroscopic outcome that‚Äôs allowed by quantum laws happens‚Äîjust in a different universe.
