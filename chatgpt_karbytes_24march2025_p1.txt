Experimental Setup

Photon source and double-slit: The source is a 351.1 nm Argon-ion laser that emits single photons. This ultraviolet photon beam is directed at a standard double-slit mask. Each photon passes through the double-slit, creating two possible paths (often labeled slit A and slit B, separated by ~0.7 mm in the experiment). Up to this point, the setup resembles a typical two-slit experiment: the photon could go through slit A, slit B, or (quantum mechanically) a superposition of both. Immediately after the slits, however, the emerging photon is not detected on a screen; instead it encounters a non-linear optical crystal that initiates the entangled photon-pair generation.

SPDC crystal (entangled photon pair generation): Just behind the slits, a beta barium borate (Î²-BaBâ‚‚Oâ‚„, BBO) non-linear crystal is placed so that the photon from either slit enters it. The crystal is oriented for type-II spontaneous parametric down-conversion (SPDC). In SPDC, one â€œpumpâ€ photon is absorbed in the crystal and converted into two lower-energy photons (the pair) that share the original photonâ€™s energy and momentum in a quantum-correlated way. In this experiment, the 351 nm pump photon is converted into two entangled photons of 702.2 nm wavelength (visible red) â€“ each new photon has about half the frequency (double the wavelength) of the pump. The two photons are produced simultaneously at the same spot in the crystal (either in region A or region B depending on which slit the pump went through) and have orthogonal polarization due to type-II phase-matching. We label these two photons as follows: photon 1 is the signal and photon 2 is the idler. Notably, the creation process entangles the photons with the slit path: if the pair came from region A, both photons are associated with slit A; if from region B, they are associated with slit B. However, because they emerge together and overlap spatially, an observer of the signal photon alone cannot directly tell which slit was involved â€“ that info resides in the two-photon entangled state.

Prism and beam splitter setup: After generation, the twin photons travel in slightly different directions due to their polarization. A Glanâ€“Thompson prism (a polarization prism) is placed just after the crystal to separate the entangled pair by polarization. The prism sends the vertically polarized photon one way and the horizontally polarized photon another way. By design, one polarization is designated the â€œsignalâ€ path (heading toward detector D0) and the other is the â€œidlerâ€ path (heading toward a different array of optical elements). Thus, immediately after the prism, the entangled photons physically split: the signal photon travels toward the D0 detector (analogous to a movable screen where interference can be observed), and the idler photon travels in a separate direction toward a series of mirrors and beam splitters rather than directly to a detector.

On the signal side, a lens (LS) is placed in front of D0 to focus the light and achieve the far-field (Fraunhofer) condition in a short distance. This means D0 effectively sits at the Fourier transform plane of the double-slit, where interference fringes would form if the light has coherence. Detector D0 itself is a single-photon counting detector that can scan along the x-axis (horizontally) via a step-motor control. By scanning D0 across different positions, one can record the distribution of signal photon hits as a function of position (to see if an interference pattern emerges over time).

On the idler side, the optical setup is more involved. The idler photon first encounters a prism labeled PS (see figure) which deflects the idler into one of two paths depending on whether it came from region A or region B. In other words, prism PS separates the idlerâ€™s trajectory based on the photon's origin (slit A vs. slit B), increasing the spatial separation between the two possible idler paths. After this point, the experiment sets up two symmetric interferometer arms for the idler, often called the â€œupperâ€ path (corresponding to slit Aâ€™s region) and the â€œlowerâ€ path (corresponding to slit Bâ€™s region). Each arm contains a 50:50 beam splitter (labeled BSA for the upper/A path and BSB for the lower/B path) and a mirror (MA or MB) further down the line. These serve to either send the idler toward one of two â€œwhich-pathâ€ detectors (D3 or D4) or to merge the paths later at a final beam splitter, as explained next. Finally, the two arms converge at a common 50:50 beam splitter BS (sometimes called BSC in descriptions) where the upper and lower idler paths are recombined (this is the quantum eraser beam splitter). The outputs of this final beam splitter go to two more detectors (D1 and D2). In total there are thus four detectors for idler photons: two (D3, D4) are placed such that they individually tell us which path the idler took, and two (D1, D2) are placed at the outputs of the recombining beam splitter to erase the path information by mixing the paths.

All five detectors (D0 for signal, D1â€“D4 for idlers) are connected to a coincidence counter circuit. This electronic device registers an event only when D0 and one of the idler detectors click simultaneously (within a set time window). By gating on coincidences, the experiment can correlate each signal photon detection at D0 with the corresponding idler detection (D1, D2, D3, or D4) from the same entangled pair. The coincidence logic is crucial for extracting meaningful interference patterns, as we will see, because it filters out unrelated photons and allows examining subsets of signals grouped by idler outcome.

Step-by-Step Sequence of Photon Events

To clarify the dynamics, here is a stepwise sequence of what happens for each entangled photon pair produced in the experiment:

Pump photon through double-slit: A single 351 nm pump photon travels through the double-slit (with slits A and B). Quantum-mechanically, the photon has a probability amplitude to pass through slit A or slit B (or both paths in superposition). No measurement is made at the slits, so the photon's path is undefined at this point (it retains wave-like potential to interfere).

1. SPDC emission of an entangled pair: Immediately after the slits, the photon enters the BBO nonlinear crystal. If the photon interacts with the crystal, it is annihilated and spontaneously down-converts into an entangled pair (signal + idler) at the site of slit A or B where the pump photon was. This process yields two 702 nm photons (signal and idler) with orthogonal polarizations. The pair originates either from region A or region B of the crystal, corresponding to which slit the pump photon took. 2. Separation of signal and idler photons: Upon exiting the crystal, the entangled photons enter the Glanâ€“Thompson prism. This prism cleanly splits the two photons by polarization.
